General Test-Taking Suggestions

  1. Use your time wisely: Calculate how much time you have to answer every question on the test and how much time you wish to allocate to each section.
  2. Answer practice questions ahead of time: After you become generally familiar with all question types, use the practice answering sample questions to prepare for the actual test. It may be useful to time yourself as you answer the practice questions to get an idea of how long you will have for each question when you sit for the actual test, as well as to determine whether you are answering quickly enough to finish the test in the allotted time.
  3. Read all the test directions carefully
  4. Read each question carefully and thoughtfully: Never skim a question or the possible answers; skimming may cause you to miss important information or nuances.
  5. Do not spend too much time on any one question: If you do not know the correct answer, or if the question is too time consuming, try to eliminate choices you know are wrong, select the best of the remaining answer choices, and move on to the next question.


Mathematics

Arithmetic

  1. Properties of Integers
    1. An integer is any number in the set {. . . −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, . . .}.
    2. Divisibility: Considering \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{x}{y} = n \end{split} \end{equation} we may say
      1. y is a divisor (factor) of x
      2. x is said to be divisible (multiple) by y
      Additionally, considering \begin{equation} \begin{split} y = xq + r ~~and~~ 0\leq r < x \end{split} \end{equation} we may say
      1. q is a quotient
      2. r is a remainder
      3. The remainder may be only smaller than or equal to the divisor
    3. Odd and Even:
      1. Any integer that is divisible by 2 is an even integer; the set of even integers is {. . . −4, −2, 0, 2, 4, 6, 8, . . .}.
      2. Integers that are not divisible by 2 are odd integers; {. . . −3, −1, 1, 3, 5, . . .} is the set of odd integers.
      3. If two integers are both even or both odd, then their sum and their difference are even. Otherwise, their sum and their difference are odd.
      4. If at least one factor of a product of integers is even, then the product is even; otherwise the product is odd.
    4. Prime Numbers:
      1. A prime number is a positive integer that has exactly two different positive divisors, 1 and itself.
      2. The number 1 is not a prime number since it has only one positive divisor.
      3. Every integer greater than 1 either is prime or can be uniquely expressed as a product of prime factors.
    5. Integer Series
      1. The numbers −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 are consecutive integers. Consecutive integers can be represented by n, n + 1, n + 2, n + 3, . . ., where n is an integer.
      2. The numbers 0, 2, 4, 6, 8 are consecutive even integers and can be represented by 2 n , 2 n + 2, 2 n + 4 , . . ., where n is an integer
      3. The numbers 3, 5, 7, 9 are consecutive odd integers and can be represented by 2 n + 1, 2 n + 3, 2 n + 5 , . . ., where n is an integer
    6. Properties of integer 1
      1. Multiplying or dividing an expression by 1, in any form, does not change the value of that expression.
      2. Dividing a number by itself results in 1 for any number n ≠ 0.
    7. Properties of integer 0: The integer 0 is neither positive nor negative. If n is any number, then n + 0 = n and n ⋅ 0 = 0. Division by 0 is not defined.
  2. Fractions
    1. A fraction is of the form \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{n}{d} \end{split} \end{equation} where n is the numerator and d is the denominator.
    2. Two fractions are said to be equivalent if they represent the same fractions when reduced to the lowest terms by their Greatest Common Divisor (GCD)
    3. Addition and Subtraction of Fractions: Two fractions with the same denominator can be added or subtracted by performing the required operation with the numerators, leaving the denominators the same. For the new denominator, choosing the least common multiple (lcm) of the denominators usually lessens the work.
    4. Multiplication and Division of Fractions:
      1. To multiply two fractions, simply multiply the two numerators and multiply the two denominators.
      2. To divide by a fraction, invert the divisor (that is, find its reciprocal) and multiply. For example, \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{2}{3} \div \frac{4}{7} = \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{7}{4} = \frac{14}{12} = \frac{7}{6} \end{split} \end{equation}
    5. Mixed Fractions: A number that consists of a whole number and a fraction.
      For example, a mixed fraction only means \begin{equation} \begin{split} 7\frac{2}{3}=7+\frac{2}{3} \end{split} \end{equation} To change a mixed number into a fraction, multiply the whole number by the denominator of the fraction and add this number to the numerator of the fraction; then put the result over the denominator of the fraction. For example, \begin{equation} \begin{split} 7\frac{2}{3}=\frac{(7\times 3) + 2}{3}=\frac{23}{3} \end{split} \end{equation}
  3. Decimals: Numbers in the decimal system are expressed in powers of 10. For example, \begin{equation} \begin{split} 7654.321=(7\times1000)+(6\times100)+(5\times10)+4+\frac{3}{10}+\frac{2}{100}+\frac{1}{1000} \end{split} \end{equation} Sometimes decimals are expressed as the product of a number with only one digit to the left of the decimal point and a power of 10. This is called scientific notation. For example \begin{equation} \begin{split} 231=2.31\times10^{2} \end{split} \end{equation} \begin{equation} \begin{split} 0.0231=2.31\times10^{-2} \end{split} \end{equation} The decimal point is moved to the right if the exponent is positive and to the left if the exponent is negative.
    1. Addition and Subtraction of Decimals: To add or subtract two decimals, the decimal points of both numbers should be lined up. If one of the numbers has fewer digits to the right of the decimal point than the other, zeros may be inserted to the right of the last digit. \begin{array}{l r} & 17.6512\\ +& 653.2700\\ \hline & 670.9212 \end{array} or \begin{array}{l r} & 653.2700\\ -& 17.6512\\ \hline & 635.6188 \end{array}
    2. Multiplication of Decimals: To multiply decimals, multiply the numbers as if they were whole numbers and then insert the decimal point in the product so that the number of digits to the right of the decimal point is equal to the sum of the numbers of digits to the right of the decimal points in the numbers being multiplied.
      For eg. in the example below, we have a number with 02 decimal places multiplied with another number having 01 decimal place resulting in a number having 03 decimal digits. \begin{array}{l r} & 2.09\\ \times& 1.3\\ \hline & 2.717 \end{array}
    3. Division of Decimals: To divide a number (the dividend) by a decimal (the divisor), move the decimal point of the divisor to the right until the divisor is a whole number. Then move the decimal point of the dividend the same number of places to the right, and divide as you would by a whole number. Apply the decimal to the quotient as the first decimal digit from the dividend is carried down for division.
      For eg. \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{698.12}{12.4}=\frac{6981.2}{124} \end{split} \end{equation}
      So \begin{array}{r} \underline{0056.3} \\[-2pt] 124 | 6981.2 \\ \underline{620\phantom{0}} \\ 781 \\ \underline{\phantom{0}744} \\ 372 \\ \underline{\phantom{0}372} \\ 0 \end{array}
  4. Real Numbers
    1. All real numbers correspond to points on the number line and all points on the number line correspond to real numbers.
    2. For any two numbers on the number line, the number to the left is less than the number to the right
    3. The distance between a number and zero on the number line is called the absolute value of the number. Thus 3 and −3 have the same absolute value, 3, since they are both three units from zero. The absolute value of 3 is denoted |3|.
    4. Some properties of real numbers are as given below:
      1. Commutative Property of Addition and Multiplication: x + y = y + x and xy = yx.
        For example, 8 + 3 = 3 + 8 = 11, and (l7)(5) = (5)(l7) = 85.
      2. Associative Property of Addition and Multiplication: (x + y) + z = x + (y + z) and (xy) z = x (yz).
        For example, (7 + 5) + 2 = 7 + (5 + 2) = 7 + (7) = 14, and (5√3)(√3) = (5)(√3√3) = (5)(3) = 15
      3. Distributive Property: xy + xz = x (y + z).
        For example, 718(36) + 718(64) = 718(36 + 64) = 718(l00) = 71,800.
      4. If x and y are both positive, then x + y and xy are positive.
      5. If x and y are both negative, then x + y is negative and xy is positive.
      6. If x is positive and y is negative, then xy is negative.
      7. If xy = 0, then x = 0 or y = 0. For example, 3 y = 0 implies y = 0.
      8. |x + y| ≤ |x| + |y|.
        For example, if x = 10 and y = 2, then | x + y | = |12| = 12 = | x | + | y |; and if x = 10 and y = −2, then | x + y | = |8| = 8 < 12 = | x | + | y |.
  5. Ratio and Proportion
    1. The ratio of the number a to the number b ( b ≠ 0) is \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{a}{b} \end{split} \end{equation}
    2. A proportion is a statement that two ratios are equal; for example, \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{2}{3}=\frac{8}{12} \end{split} \end{equation}
    3. One way to solve a proportion involving an unknown is to cross multiply, obtaining a new equality.
      For example, to solve for n in the proportion \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{2}{3}=\frac{n}{12} \end{split} \end{equation} cross multiply the two, obtaining 24 = 3n; then divide both sides by 3, to get n = 8.
  6. Percentage: means per hundred or number out of 100. A percent can be represented as a fraction with a denominator of 100, or as a decimal. For example: \begin{equation} \begin{split} 37\%=\frac{37}{100}=0.37 \end{split} \end{equation}
    1. To find a certain percent of a number, multiply the number by the percent expressed as a decimal or fraction. For example:
      20% of 90 = 0.2 x 90 = 18
      or
      \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{20}{100}\times90=\frac{1}{5}\times90=18 \end{split} \end{equation}
    2. Percents greater than 100% are represented by numbers greater than 1 while percents smaller than 100% are represented by numbers smaller than 1.
    3. Percent Change: To find the percent increase, first find the amount of the increase; then divide this increase by the original amount, and express this quotient as a percent.
      For example, “If the price of an item increases from $24 to $30, what is the percent increase in price?”
      In the example above, the percent increase would be found in the following way:
      \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{Original Price - New Price}{Original Price}=\frac{Price \hspace{0.15cm} Difference}{Original Price} = \frac{30-24}{24}=\frac{6}{24}=25\% \end{split} \end{equation}
      A similar calculation for percent decrease from $30 to $24 would be as below: \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{Original Price - New Price}{Original Price}=\frac{Price \hspace{0.15cm} Difference}{Original Price} = \frac{30-24}{30}=\frac{6}{30}=20\% \end{split} \end{equation}
    4. HOTS: If the cost of a certain house in 1983 was 300 percent of its cost in 1970, by what percent did the cost increase?
      If n is the cost in 1970, then the percent increase is equal to \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{3n - n}{n}=\frac{2n}{n} = 2\hspace{0.15cm} or\hspace{0.15cm} 200\% \end{split} \end{equation}
  7. Power and Roots of Numbers: When a number k is to be used n times as a factor in a product, it can be expressed as kn , which means the nth power of k.
    For example, 22 = 2 × 2 = 4 and 23> = 2 × 2 × 2 = 8 are powers of 2.
    A square root of a number n is a number that, when squared, is equal to n.
    1. Squaring a number that is greater than 1, or raising it to a higher power, results in a larger number; squaring a number between 0 and 1 results in a smaller number.
    2. The square root of a negative number is not a real number.
    3. Every positive number n has two square roots, one positive and the other negative, but n denotes the positive number whose square is n.
      For example, √9 denotes 3. The two square roots of 9 are 9 = 3 and − 3.
  8. Descriptive Statistics: A list of numbers, or numerical data, can be described by various statistical measures, as explained below:
    1. Average, or Arithmetic Mean: which locates a type of “center” for the data. The average of n numbers is defined as the sum of the n numbers divided by n.
      For example, the average of 6, 4, 7, 10, and 4 is \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{6 + 4 + 7 + 10 + 4}{5}=\frac{31}{5}=6.2 \end{split} \end{equation}
    2. Median: is another type of center for a list of numbers. To calculate the median of n numbers, first order the numbers from least to greatest; then
      1. if n is odd, the median is defined as the middle number
      2. if n is even, the median is defined as the average of the two middle numbers.
      In the example above,
      1. The numbers arranged in ascending order are 4, 4, 6, 7, 10
      2. Since the number of digits are 5 (odd), the median would be the middle digit, that is 6.
      For the numbers 4, 6, 6, 8, 9, 12, the median would be \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{(\frac{n}{2})+(\frac{n}{2}+1)}{2}=\frac{6+8}{2}=7 \end{split} \end{equation}
    3. Mode: of a list of numbers is the number that occurs most frequently in the list.
      For example, the mode of 1, 3, 6, 4, 3, 5 is 3.
      A list of numbers may have more than one mode.
      For example, the list 1, 2, 3, 3, 3, 5, 7, 10, 10, 10, 20 has two modes, 3 and 10.
    4. Statistical Measures of Dispersion: are as below:
      1. Range: The simplest measure of dispersion is the range, which is defined as the greatest value in the numerical data minus the least value.
        For example, the range of 11, 10, 5, 13, 21 is 21 − 5 = 16.
        Note how the range depends on only two values in the data.
      2. Standard Deviation: Generally speaking, the more the data are spread away from the mean, the greater the standard deviation. The standard deviation of n numbers can be calculated as follows:
        1. Order the digits, x
        2. Evaluate for n, the number of digits
        3. Evaluate for x̄, arithmetic mean of the digits in consideration
        4. Tabulate and find (x-x̄) for every x
        5. Find (x-x̄)2 for every x
        6. Find ∑(x-x̄)2/ n
        7. Find √ of the average computed above
        For eg. standard deviation calculation of the numbers 0, 7, 8, 10, 10 is as below:
        1. Digits are 0, 7, 8, 10, 10
        2. n = 5
        3. Arithmetic Mean, x̄ equals \begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{0+7+8+10+10}{5}=\frac{35}{5}=7 \end{split} \end{equation}
        4. x (x-x̄) (x-x̄)2
          0 -7 49
          7 0 0
          8 1 1
          10 3 9
          10 3 9
          ∑x = 68
        5. \begin{equation} \begin{split} \sqrt{\frac{68}{5}}=\sqrt{13.6}=3.68 \end{split} \end{equation}
        The standard deviation depends on every data value, although it depends most on values that are farthest from the mean. This is why a distribution with data grouped closely around the mean will have a smaller standard deviation than will data spread far from the mean.
    5. Statistical Measures of Distribution: There are many ways to display numerical data that show how the data are distributed. One simple way is with a frequency distribution, which is useful for data that have values occurring with varying frequencies.
